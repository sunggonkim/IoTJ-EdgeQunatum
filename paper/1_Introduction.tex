\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The convergence of quantum computing and Internet of Things (IoT) presents a transformative opportunity for next-generation intelligent systems. While cloud-based quantum computers such as IBM Quantum~\cite{ibmq} and Google Sycamore~\cite{arute2019quantum} offer unprecedented computational power, latency constraints, data privacy concerns, and intermittent connectivity in edge environments motivate the exploration of edge-based quantum simulation. This paper addresses a fundamental question: \textit{Can resource-constrained IoT edge devices effectively simulate variational quantum algorithms for practical optimization tasks?}

\subsection{Motivation: The Edge Quantum Gap}

Modern IoT deployments span smart grids, autonomous vehicles, and industrial automation, where real-time optimization decisions must be made locally without round-trip latency to cloud quantum services. Consider a smart grid scenario: optimal power flow calculations require solving quadratic unconstrained binary optimization (QUBO) problems that are naturally suited for quantum approximate optimization algorithms (QAOA). However, current cloud quantum access exhibits 100ms--10s latency~\cite{aws-braket}, rendering it unsuitable for millisecond-scale grid control decisions.

Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs) represent a promising class of hybrid quantum-classical algorithms designed for near-term quantum devices~\cite{cerezo2021variational}. The Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE)~\cite{peruzzo2014variational} finds ground state energies for molecular Hamiltonians, while QAOA~\cite{farhi2014quantum} addresses combinatorial optimization. Both employ parameterized quantum circuits optimized through classical feedback loops, making them amenable to GPU-accelerated classical simulation.

\subsection{Motivational Evaluation}

To quantify the feasibility of edge quantum simulation, we conducted preliminary experiments on an NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano (8GB unified memory, 15W power envelope). Figure~\ref{fig:motivation} summarizes our findings.

\textbf{Qubit Scalability.} We systematically increased the qubit count from 20 to 30 qubits until memory exhaustion. Table~\ref{tab:motivation_scaling} presents the results:

The Jetson Orin Nano successfully simulates \textbf{26 qubits} (67 million complex amplitudes) with a single Hadamard gate applied in 0.44 seconds. At 27 qubits, the Linux OOM killer terminates the process due to unified memory exhaustion (1GB state vector + intermediate allocations exceeding 8GB total). This establishes a practical upper bound for edge quantum simulation.

\subsection{Contributions}

This paper makes the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{EdgeQuantum Framework:} A GPU-accelerated quantum circuit simulator optimized for ARM-based edge devices (NVIDIA Jetson platform), featuring memory-efficient tensor-based gate application.
    
    \item \textbf{Scalability Analysis:} Comprehensive benchmarks demonstrating VQE/QAOA performance from 4 to 26 qubits on edge hardware, establishing the practical limits of edge quantum simulation.
    
    \item \textbf{Memory-Efficient QAOA:} Novel diagonal operator formulation for QAOA cost Hamiltonians, eliminating matrix storage and reducing GPU memory by 10$\times$.
    
    \item \textbf{Open-Source Release:} Full implementation available at \url{https://github.com/sunggonkim/IoTJ-EdgeQuantum}.
\end{enumerate}
